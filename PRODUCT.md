# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Architecture firms and studios: hiring managers, project leads, and senior architects evaluating candidates for internships and entry-level positions. They have seen hundreds of portfolios and will immediately recognize when a site is just a template. They are looking for evidence of a design sensibility, not a checklist of projects.

## Product Purpose

Aayush Mondkar's portfolio — trained as an architect, wired as an artist. The site moves across architecture, identity, print, and digital without treating any field as secondary. Success is a visitor who remembers the site as an experience, not just a container for work. The ASCII eyes, the typewriter headline, the pixel display font are not decorations; they are the argument.

## Brand Personality

Playful, poetic, curious. Bold enough to ignore portfolio conventions entirely. The energy of a music artist's personal site: idiosyncratic, identity-first, not apologetic. The site should feel like encountering a person, not browsing a directory.

## Anti-references

- Typical architecture portfolio sites: neutral Swiss grids, full-bleed image heroes, project numbers as section markers, the restrained-professional affect that reads as confident but is actually fearful.
- Flashy SPA scroll storytelling: pinned sections, parallax, scroll-triggered reveals that make the interaction the product rather than the work.
- Generic Behance / Cargo templates: card grids, soft shadows, rounded corners, any visual language the visitor has absorbed from 200 other portfolios.

## Design Principles

1. Personality over convention. The site should feel like a person, not a portfolio. If a choice is safe, it's probably wrong.
2. Every element earns its strangeness. The cursor-tracking ASCII eyes, the Coral Pixels font, the typewriter — these are voice, not decoration. Any new element must clear the same bar.
3. Restraint as a held note. The black/white palette holds. Accent color is not an option; tension comes from the collision of Coral Pixels with Helvetica Neue Bold, not from hue.
4. Hierarchy of attention. Images lead; text frames. On project pages, the work is the center — the UI is the quiet room around it.
5. Curious at every scale. Details reward close attention. Nothing should be throwaway — even a caption, a separator, a hover state.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA as a baseline. Reduced-motion alternatives for all animations (the typewriter and ASCII-eye render as static on prefers-reduced-motion). Contrast: body text (#121212 on #ffffff) is fine; muted text (#6b6b68 on #ffffff) passes at ~4.6:1 and should not be lightened further.
